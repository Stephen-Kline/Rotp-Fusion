# CONTEXT.md

## Glossary

**Civilizational Arc** — the core game structure; the player grows a civilization from a single planet (Earth, 1960) to a Type III Kardashev civilization over millennia.

**Kardashev Progression** — the primary macro-structure of the game. Each Kardashev tier (K1: planetary, K2: stellar, K3: galactic) is a distinct era with different scale, mechanics, and tensions.

**Cosmic Timescale** — the game runs in real-time but time can be compressed (years, decades, centuries per second). The timescale expands exponentially as the civilization grows.

**Governor Model** — the default mode of play at scale; the player sets high-level policy and the civilization executes autonomously. The player intervenes at key moments (crises, discoveries, diplomacy).

**Director** — what the player is; not a micromanager but a high-level decision-maker who can drop into direct control (placing structures, issuing orders) at any time.

**Primary Tension** — time vs. readiness; the player is always deciding whether to push to the next milestone before they're fully prepared, or consolidate and risk being blindsided.

**Escalator** — known vs. unknown; the universe contains strange things (big dumb objects, alien races) that will eventually find the player whether they're ready or not.

**Big Dumb Object (BDO)** — a mysterious large structure or phenomenon encountered during exploration; nature and intent unknown at discovery.

**Alternate Timeline** — the game begins in 1960 in a world where the space race never ended; Earth must first be unified before interstellar expansion is possible.

**Government Type** — the mechanical backbone of civilization identity; shapes decision speed, unity, and available policy options. Can evolve over time.

**Founding Principles** — values crystallized during the Earth unification arc (e.g. militaristic, cooperative, expansionist, isolationist); create persistent bonuses, penalties, and narrative flavor.

**Cultural Drift** — the natural fragmentation of civilization identity as it spreads across space and time; the source of late-game internal tension.

**Rebel Faction** — a splinter group that has drifted far enough from the civilization's identity to actively resist central authority; can range from political dissent to armed insurrection to full secession.

**Alien Civilization** — procedurally generated races with unique traits, governments, and agendas; rare anchor civilizations have deep lore and unique mechanics. Perception of alien civilizations is Kardashev-gated — a K1 civilization may not be able to comprehend what a K3 civilization even is. Contact probability is low at K1 regardless of whether other civilizations exist — space is too vast. Detection and contact likelihood scale with Kardashev tier and explored volume of the galaxy. The Fermi paradox is a felt early-game reality, not a background assumption.

**Kardashev-Gated Perception** — what the player can perceive, understand, or interact with is bounded by their current Kardashev tier. Higher-tier phenomena are invisible or incomprehensible until the player reaches sufficient development.

**Human Divergence** — over millennia of separation, low gravity, radiation, genetic engineering, or deliberate cultural choice, human populations splinter into distinct subspecies or clades. These are neither fully alien nor fully human — a source of identity tension, diplomacy complexity, and potential conflict. Divergent clades develop independently and may return at a wildly different Kardashev tier than the civilization they split from.

**Lost Clade** — a human splinter group that went silent and disappeared, potentially for thousands or tens of thousands of years. When rediscovered, they may be unrecognizable — biologically, culturally, technologically. A Lost Clade returning at a higher Kardashev tier than the player is one of the game's most dramatic possible events.

**Existential Stakes** — the true endgame; something in the universe poses an ultimate threat or transformation that the player doesn't fully understand at the start. Discovering its nature is part of the known vs. unknown escalator. It recontextualizes everything built before it.

**Player-Defined Milestone** — a personal victory condition set at game start (e.g. peaceful galactic unification, last civilization standing, first contact with a specific phenomenon). Milestones are achieved mid-game; the existential stakes are the true finish line.

**Existential Threat** — selected at game start from a curated set; each produces a fundamentally different late game. Examples: cosmic phenomenon, K4+ civilization, a consequence of humanity's own expansion, or the BDOs awakening. The player may not know which threat they face until it begins to reveal itself.

**Visual Language** — stylized sci-fi, layered by era. Early game is grounded at Earth scale; as the player reaches orbit and beyond, the camera scales out and the visual language shifts toward abstraction. Placeholder art is intentional — final aesthetic to be defined with an artist. Do not treat current visuals as canonical.

**Communication Lag** — handwaved for playability; a short lag exists early game as flavor/constraint, researchable technology (e.g. ansible) eventually enables instantaneous comms. Lag is a real military and economic pressure before that tech is available.

**Energy Capacity** — a primary economic indicator tracking a civilization's total energy production; gates construction, research, and population growth. Progression mirrors Kardashev tier advancement.

**Tech Tree** — Civ-style tiered research system. Significant depth within each Kardashev tier; crossing tiers requires difficult prerequisite research. Exotic propulsion, megastructures, and instantaneous comms are all tech-tree gated.

**Propulsion Tier** — the available means of interstellar travel at a given Kardashev level. Early: generation ships, conventional rockets. Mid: exotic propulsion (warp, spacetime bending). Late: stargates, wormholes. Speed of light is a hard constraint until exotic propulsion is researched.

**Generation Ship** — a valid low-tier interstellar vessel; crew lives and dies over the voyage. Available before FTL is researched. The colony that arrives may be culturally and biologically different from the one that departed — a seed of Human Divergence.

**Megastructure** — large-scale engineered constructs unlocked by late-tier research: Dyson spheres, stargates, black hole harnesses, orbital rings. Each dramatically alters energy capacity or propulsion capability.

**Macro Economy** — four pillars: food/infrastructure (supports population), education (supports research output), industry (supports construction), energy (gates everything). All four must scale together as the civilization grows. Over-investing in any one pillar starves the others — the core economic balancing act.

**Research Rate** — determined by education output, civilizational unity, and economic allocation to research. Accelerated by time compression. A highly unified, research-focused civilization advances faster but may be unable to support its population or build infrastructure.

**Population Unit** — an abstracted grouping of people at scale-appropriate magnitude. One unit in 1960 represents millions; one unit in the year 12000 may represent billions or trillions. The player always reads the same kind of number regardless of era. Units are the basis for labor, military, and economic output calculations.

**Terraforming** — a long-duration background process initiated by the player and accelerated by placed infrastructure. Not actively managed once started; rewards early investment with faster habitability. Duration spans decades to centuries of game time depending on planet type and infrastructure level.

**Universe Map** — hybrid star map. Real star catalog (e.g. HYG database) for the stellar neighborhood within a few hundred light-years of Earth; procedurally generated beyond that following accurate Milky Way arm structure and density. The seam is invisible to the player. Full galactic scale is not visible at K1 — reveals progressively as the civilization expands.

**Drill-Down View** — the UI model for managing locations. The player's default late-game view is the galaxy map; they can zoom into any star system, then any planet, to place structures or review status. Early game is planet-scale by default. Late game, system-level views are available but not required — the governor handles routine management.

**Structure Upgrade** — manual by default; the player initiates upgrades and sets priorities. Can be switched to automated per-structure, per-planet, or globally, allowing the governor to upgrade when prerequisites and resources allow. Manual and automated can coexist — the player retains override at any time.

**Colony Maturity** — as a colony becomes self-sufficient, it gains autonomy in the governor model, becomes a source of cultural drift if neglected, and can found its own sub-colonies. Which of these dominates depends on governance choices: investing in population happiness suppresses drift but has economic costs. Governance style has compounding downstream consequences across centuries.

**Energy Locality** — at K1 and early K2, energy is produced and consumed locally; interstellar energy transfer is physically impossible without exotic infrastructure. As the civilization advances, energy networks can span systems and eventually the galaxy via stargates, energy beaming, or equivalent tech. A K1 colony at Tau Ceti cannot draw energy from Earth.

**K1 Technology Constraint** — K1 technology must be grounded in current science or near-future extrapolation. Theoretical but physically plausible technologies (nuclear pulse drives, space elevators, orbital rings) are allowed. Exotic or speculative physics (FTL, warp) are not available until higher Kardashev tiers.

**Victory Paths** — the primary paths to winning are cultural dominance, scientific achievement, and exploration/discovery. Military conquest is possible but requires advancing through Kardashev tiers and achieving significant economic dominance to sustain military industry — a committed path with lasting consequences.

**Cultural Victory** — achieved by spreading your civilization's values across the galaxy until you become the dominant cultural force, or by reaching K4. K4 represents transcendence — becoming something beyond the game's starting frame, the ultimate endpoint regardless of victory path.

**Scientific Victory** — achieved through any of: (1) completing the tech tree and achieving total scientific mastery; (2) making a specific reality-reframing discovery (the edge of the universe, control over spacetime, multiverse access) — these are K4-adjacent and truly game-ending; (3) becoming the scientific leader that other civilizations defer to. FTL is not a game-ending discovery — it is mid-game infrastructure. BDO decoding is a candidate for a specific discovery victory, but may also trigger the existential threat depending on what the BDO is.

**Exploration Victory** — achieved by meeting thresholds across three dimensions: (1) cartographic — mapping most of the galaxy; (2) contact-based — first contact with every civilization, BDO, and anomaly; (3) discovery-based — finding and documenting specific wonders and phenomena scattered through the galaxy. Requires sustained commitment across the full game arc, not a late-game sprint.

**Cosmic Events** — random and scheduled events driven by the universe's own evolution at cosmic timescale. Includes stellar events (supernovae, neutron star formation, gamma ray bursts), stellar lifecycle events (the Sun expanding into a red giant and eventually destroying Earth), and other large-scale phenomena. Scheduled events like Earth's destruction are on a known astronomical timeline; the player must have solved civilizational redundancy before they arrive or face catastrophic consequences. Some cosmic events are consequences of player actions — destabilizing a system through over-mining, triggering a BDO response, or a megastructure experiment gone wrong.

**Military Conquest** — a valid but difficult win path. Requires Kardashev advancement and economic dominance to produce sufficient military industry. Pursuing it has downstream consequences (diplomatic damage, internal dissent, resource drain) that make it the hardest path to sustain.

**Diplomacy** — manageable by default (Civ-style); difficulty is determined by relationship history (wars, harm caused) and Kardashev gap. A two-tier Kardashev gap may mean one civilization doesn't recognize the other as a meaningful diplomatic peer — communication itself becomes a research problem. Treating other civilizations well reduces diplomatic friction; exploiting or attacking them increases it permanently.
